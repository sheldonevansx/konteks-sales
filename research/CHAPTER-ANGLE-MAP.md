# Konteks Kourse - Chapter-Based Ad Angle Map

## Chapter 01: "So... Architecture"
**What it teaches:** What architecture school actually expects from you. How to read the room before you pick up a pen.
**Specific topics:**
- How the grading system really works
- What lecturers actually look for (the hidden rubric)
- How to read the room before you start designing

**BEFORE state (pain):**
- You have no idea what lecturers want
- You get vague feedback like "push it further" and don't know what it means
- You start projects blind, guessing what's expected
- You get shocked by grades because you don't understand the criteria
- First-years feel completely lost in studio

**AFTER state (desired):**
- You understand exactly what lecturers grade on
- You know what "push it further" actually means
- You start every project knowing the expectations
- Your grades reflect your effort because you're designing for the right criteria

**Ad angles from this chapter:**
1. "Your lecturers grade you on a rubric they never share. I'll show you the rubric."
2. "'Push it further' isn't feedback. It's a confession that they can't teach you what's missing."
3. "First years: here's what nobody tells you in orientation."
4. "The grading system has rules. Your lecturers just never wrote them down."
5. "You're not bad at design. You're designing for the wrong criteria."

## Chapter 02: "The Top Student Mindset"
**What it teaches:** Abstract thinking, creative risk-taking, and how to stop killing your best ideas before they exist.
**Specific topics:**
- Why "being silly" is a serious design skill
- The iteration method top students use
- How to stop filtering ideas too early

**BEFORE state (pain):**
- You censor your ideas before they're even developed
- You play it safe because risky concepts feel dangerous
- Your concepts are generic ("light and shadow", "connection to nature")
- You think creativity is talent, not a learnable skill
- You start with Pinterest and copy what looks good

**AFTER state (desired):**
- You generate wild ideas and know how to refine them
- You take creative risks that make lecturers pay attention
- Your concepts are specific and unique to you
- You understand creativity as a PROCESS, not a gift
- You develop original thinking that stands out in crits

**Ad angles from this chapter:**
1. "The top students aren't more talented. They just stopped censoring themselves in week 1."
2. "Your best idea is the one you killed 5 minutes after having it."
3. "Every student starts on Pinterest. The good ones leave."
4. "'Light and shadow' is not a concept. Here's how to find one that is."
5. "Creativity isn't a talent. It's a method. Nobody taught you the method."

## Chapter 03: "Crits, Deadlines & Timing"
**What it teaches:** The time management system that got Dan top student in Theory AND Professional Practice in the same year.
**Specific topics:**
- The Deep Work / Steady Work / Easy Work framework
- How to structure your week around studio
- How to present at crits with confidence

**BEFORE state (pain):**
- You pull all-nighters regularly
- You finish projects at 3AM the night before
- Crits feel like personal attacks
- You can't manage your time across multiple subjects
- You dread presentations and freeze up

**AFTER state (desired):**
- You finish by 8PM and actually sleep
- Your projects are done 48 hours before crits
- You present confidently and handle feedback
- You juggle studio + theory + professional practice
- You topped Theory AND Professional Practice in the same year (like Dan)

**Ad angles from this chapter:**
1. "Dan topped Theory AND Professional Practice the same year. Here's her actual weekly schedule."
2. "Deep Work. Steady Work. Easy Work. Three buckets. Your whole week organized."
3. "You're not pulling all-nighters because you're slow. You're pulling them because nobody taught you to plan."
4. "Crits aren't attacks. They're conversations you haven't been trained for."
5. "The students who finish early aren't faster. They started the right tasks first."

## Chapter 04: "From Brief to Concept"
**What it teaches:** How to develop concepts so specific your lecturer asks how you thought of it.
**Specific topics:**
- Moving from generic to place-specific concepts
- The brief analysis method that reveals hidden angles
- How to present a concept that sticks

**BEFORE state (pain):**
- You stare at the brief for days and have nothing
- Your concepts are generic (nature, light, community)
- You can't explain your concept in crits
- You start designing before you have a real idea
- Every project feels like starting from scratch

**AFTER state (desired):**
- You go from brief to concept in under 48 hours
- Your concepts are so specific the lecturer asks how you thought of it
- You present concepts that stick in people's minds
- You have a repeatable method for every project
- Each project builds on a clear, defensible idea

**Ad angles from this chapter:**
1. "Brief to concept in 48 hours. Not because you're rushing. Because you have a method."
2. "Your concept isn't 'light and shadow'. That's a Pinterest caption, not an idea."
3. "The brief analysis method that reveals angles your classmates will never see."
4. "Staring at a blank page isn't designing. It's panicking without a framework."
5. "I haven't had a student stuck on a concept for more than 2 days after learning this."

## Chapter 05: "Site Analysis & Urban Design"
**What it teaches:** Beyond the sun path. How to understand a place so deeply your project can only belong there.
**Specific topics:**
- Layered analysis (history, movement, social, environmental)
- Diagramming techniques that show depth
- Turning analysis into design decisions

**BEFORE state (pain):**
- Your site analysis is a sun path and wind arrows on a map
- You don't know how to turn site data into design moves
- Your diagrams look identical to everyone else's
- Your project could exist anywhere - it's not site-specific
- Lecturers say your analysis is "surface level"

**AFTER state (desired):**
- Your analysis has 4+ layers (history, movement, social, environmental)
- Your diagrams are unique and show real understanding
- Your project can only exist on THAT site
- Analysis directly drives design decisions
- Lecturers cite your analysis as an example to other students

**Ad angles from this chapter:**
1. "Your site analysis has one layer. Theirs has four. That's the entire difference."
2. "Sun path. Wind arrows. North arrow. Is that really your site analysis?"
3. "If your project could exist on any site, your analysis failed."
4. "The diagramming technique that makes lecturers say 'now THIS is site analysis.'"
5. "Turning analysis into design decisions is a skill. Not an instinct."
