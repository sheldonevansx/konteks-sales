## Part 1: Framework Catalog

### 1. Three-Part Hook Anatomy Framework
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
Every hook consists of three distinct elements that must work together:

1. **Audio** - What is being said/the underlying sound
2. **Copy** - Text on the ad (not primary text, but on-screen text/headlines)
3. **Visual** - The scroll-stopper imagery/movement

**How it works:**  
You can have a good hook concept, but if ONE of these three elements isn't dialed in, the hook will underperform. Changing just one element (e.g., adding bold copy overlay "THIS TRANSFORMED MY LIFE") can double hook rates and overall performance.

**When to use it:**  
Every time you design or critique a hook. Don't just think "visual" - systematically check all three elements.

**Nathan's example:**  
A hook performing okay suddenly doubles in performance when exaggerated copy is added on top, even though the visual and audio stayed the same.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
- **Audio:** Student speaking casually about their crit experience
- **Copy:** "The feedback I got was 'make it more architectural'" (relatable pain point)
- **Visual:** Student sitting at desk late night with model/drawings visible

### 2. Persona-Problem-Pattern Interrupt-Promise (4P Hook Framework)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A proven hook structure that filters audience, agitates pain, prevents drop-off, and gives reason to watch.

**Steps:**
1. **Persona** - Immediately filter out anyone who isn't your target ("If you're an architecture student...")
2. **Problem** - Problem agitation within first 2 seconds ("...and your prof's crit feedback is always vague...")
3. **Pattern Interrupt** - Keep people watching past the agitation point (visual change, audio shift, unexpected element)
4. **Promise** - Give them a reason to continue ("...I'm showing you the exact 3-question framework I use before every crit")

**When to use it:**  
When you need a reliable high-performing hook structure. Works for both organic and paid.

**Nathan's example:**  
"If you're an e-commerce brand struggling with creative content, I'm going to give you the best method possible for creating hooks. This will be a 20-minute video where I break down all the frameworks."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
"If you're an architecture student and you're tired of all-nighters that don't actually improve your project [PERSONA + PROBLEM], you're probably doing what everyone does - modeling before you understand the concept [PATTERN INTERRUPT - call out the mistake]. Here's the 5-minute framework that fixes it [PROMISE]."

### 3. Hook Clarity-Relevance-Novelty-Specificity-Credibility Scorecard
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A five-criteria rating system for evaluating hooks (ordered by importance).

**Criteria (in order of priority):**
1. **Clarity** - Can a stranger understand what the product is?
2. **Relevance** - Can they directly relate the pain point to themselves?
3. **Novelty** - Is there a twist/new angle/format no one's done before?
4. **Specificity** - Are you being specific in what's called out? (Lower priority)
5. **Credibility** - Is there proof or reason to believe? (Lower priority)

**When to use it:**  
After creating a hook, score it 1-10 on each criterion. Clarity and Relevance are non-negotiable. Novelty gives you white space. Specificity and Credibility are nice-to-haves.

**Nathan's example:**  
He critiques a jerky ad that had a curiosity gap (audio of product being eaten) but scored LOW on clarity and relevance because you couldn't tell what was being sold until later.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Bad hook:** "Watch this design transformation" (low clarity - what kind of design?)  
**Good hook:** "Site analysis taking you 8 hours? Here's the 45-minute method" (high clarity, high relevance, specific time frames)

### 4. Product Awareness Curve (The Bridge Framework)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A model for how product awareness should gradually increase through a video ad, NOT in sudden jumps.

**How it works:**  
- **Y-axis:** Product awareness (user knowing they're being sold to)
- **X-axis:** Time (seconds into the ad)
- **Ideal curve:** Starts at ZERO through the hook, slowly creeps up, reaches peak at CTA
- **Bad curve:** Jumps immediately (product mentioned in first 3 seconds), then plateaus, then jumps again at CTA

**When to use it:**  
When scripting video ads. The bridge between hook and body should NOT immediately sell the product. Slowly introduce it through education.

**Nathan's example:**  
Hyro electrolyte ad that says "I need to talk about Hyro" in the first sentence - immediately spikes product awareness and causes drop-off.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
Don't start with "Join Konteks Kourse." Start with education about the problem (vague crit feedback, concept paralysis), then slowly introduce "here's what I found" (the framework), THEN mention it's inside the course.

### 5. Hook-to-Body Bridge (Smooth Transition Framework)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
The most common mistake in ad scripting - going straight from hook into selling the product instead of bridging through education.

**How it works:**  
- **Hook:** Problem agitation, persona callout
- **Bridge:** Go into EDUCATION, not product pitch. Teach them something.
- **Body:** Continue education while slowly embedding product mentions
- **CTA:** Offer the product as the natural next step

**When to use it:**  
Every video ad script. The bridge is where you lose people if you jump straight to selling.

**Nathan's example:**  
"If you think agencies can't be trusted, you're not wrong. Here's how to tell if yours is the exception." Then immediately: "Book a call below and talk to my team" = BAD BRIDGE. Should go into education about what to look for in an agency FIRST.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Bad:** "Tired of vague crit feedback? Join Konteks Kourse." (no bridge)  
**Good:** "Tired of vague crit feedback? Here's what they're actually asking for when they say 'make it more architectural' - they want you to [TEACH THE FRAMEWORK], and once you get this, crit becomes way easier. This is exactly what we break down in Konteks Kourse."

### 6. Concept = Persona + Angle + Offer Framework
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A concept is NOT just the creative idea - it's the strategic combination of WHO you're targeting, WHAT pain/benefit you're highlighting, and WHAT you're offering.

**Components:**
- **Persona:** Who are you speaking to? (GLP-1 users, architecture students, etc.)
- **Angle:** What problem/benefit are you focusing on? (Hair loss, skin issues, vague feedback, concept paralysis)
- **Offer:** What are you selling? (Product SKU, bundle, discount, membership)

**How it works:**  
You build the concept FIRST (persona + angle + offer), THEN you execute it across multiple formats (UGC, static, carousel, AI video, etc.).

**When to use it:**  
Before creating any ad. Define the concept in a Google Sheet with columns: Persona | Angle | Offer | Format.

**Nathan's example:**  
**Persona:** GLP-1 users  
**Angle:** Healthy hair (GLP-1 side effect is hair loss)  
**Offer:** Gummy (single SKU)  
**Format:** UGC video

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Persona:** Architecture students (year 2-4, stressed about crits)  
**Angle:** Vague crit feedback ("make it more architectural")  
**Offer:** $29/mo Skool membership  
**Format:** Notes-style static, UGC video, carousel of student testimonials

### 7. Market Research → Concept Generation Loop
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A systematic process for generating endless concepts by researching persona pain points and mapping them to angles.

**Steps:**
1. Pick a persona (e.g., GLP-1 users)
2. Research their problems (Reddit, Facebook forums, reviews) - what OTHER issues do they face?
3. List out all problems (hair loss, skin issues, gut problems, fatigue, etc.)
4. Map each problem to an angle
5. For each angle, design a concept
6. Execute concept across formats

**When to use it:**  
When you need fresh creative concepts. Don't guess - research what the persona is actually complaining about.

**Nathan's example:**  
GLP-1 users don't just have hair problems. They have skin problems, gut problems, energy problems. Each becomes a separate concept for the same product.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
Research architecture student pain points:
- Vague crit feedback → Angle: "Decode what profs actually want"
- All-nighters → Angle: "Faster workflow systems"
- Site analysis paralysis → Angle: "The 5-step site analysis method"
- Concept development stuck → Angle: "Concept generation framework"
- Portfolio anxiety → Angle: "Portfolio that actually gets internships"

Each becomes a separate ad concept.

### 8. Format Diversity Post-Andromeda
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
Meta's Andromeda update groups similar-looking ads together and serves them to the same people. You now need format diversity, not just message diversity.

**How it works:**  
Previously: Test 100 different static ads with different text = 100 separate tests.  
Post-Andromeda: Meta sees them as "similar" and won't show them all to different people.

**Solution:** Change the FORMAT alongside the concept.
- Same message, different format = more likely to be treated as separate ads
- Don't just change copy - change visual structure (UGC → carousel → AI video → static)

**When to use it:**  
When planning creative tests. Don't make 50 static ads with slightly different text. Make 10 concepts across 5 different formats.

**Nathan's example:**  
You can test a concept 1,000 different ways (static, video, UGC, carousel, AI UGC, professional shoot) and Meta will treat them as separate.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Concept:** "Vague crit feedback decoded"  
**Formats:**
- Notes-style static (text interface)
- UGC video (student testimonial)
- Carousel (5 slides: common feedback → what it means → how to apply it)
- AI video (voiceover over student work examples)
- Before/after static (project before vs. after applying framework)

### 9. Hook Testing > Body Testing (90% Rule)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
90% of people don't make it past the hook. Therefore, 90% of your ad optimization effort should be on the hook, not the body.

**How it works:**  
Instead of making 10 completely new ads (high effort), make 10 different hooks on ONE high-performing body (low effort, high ROI).

**Steps:**
1. Find a body that's performing well
2. Create 10 different 3-5 second hooks
3. Swap them onto the same body
4. Test which hook gets best hook rate + performance

**When to use it:**  
When you have a winning ad that's starting to fatigue. Don't remake the whole ad - test new hooks on the same body.

**Nathan's example:**  
"This video probably goes for 75 seconds total. Majority of the effort in shooting and editing is in the last 73 seconds. We can make 10 ads for the same effort as making one body."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Body (winning script):** Student explaining the "3 questions to ask before crit" framework (60 seconds)  
**Hook variations:**
1. "My prof said 'it's too literal' and I had no idea what that meant"
2. "I used to spend 12 hours the night before crit and still fail"
3. "The difference between a pass and a fail is knowing these 3 questions"
4. "Crit feedback feels random because no one tells you this"
5. "Site analysis taking 8 hours? Here's the 45-minute method"

### 10. Three Hook Types That Work for Any Brand
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**Type 1: Problem-Solution Hook**
- Identify a common pain point
- Immediately offer your product as the solution
- Works for problem-aware audiences
- **Example:** "Struggling with back pain from long hours at your desk? The XYZ chair fixes it by..."
- **Konteks Kourse:** "Tired of professors saying 'make it more architectural' without explaining what that means? Here's the framework that decodes it."

**Type 2: Curiosity Hook**
- Use ambiguous/teaser language
- Create an information gap
- Leverage psychological trigger to fill in missing info
- **Example:** "Ever wonder what the top 1% know about saving money that you don't?"
- **Konteks Kourse:** "Most architecture students don't know this is why their site analysis takes 8 hours. Here's the 45-minute method."

**Type 3: Authority Hook**
- Lead with social proof immediately
- Build credibility right out of the gates
- Best for bottom-of-funnel, but works top-of-funnel too
- **Examples:**
  - "As seen on [media outlet]"
  - "Over 90% of customers saw results in the first 30 days"
  - "Discover how Jane transformed her skin within 12 days"
- **Konteks Kourse:** 
  - "Over 200 architecture students are using this crit framework"
  - "Discover how Maya went from all-nighters to finishing projects 2 days early"

### 11. Structured Testing Framework (ABO Version-Based Testing)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A systematic approach to running Meta ad tests with proper analysis and iteration.

**Structure:**
- **Campaign level:** ABO (Ad set Budget Optimization) - NOT CBO
  - Why ABO? Keeps optimization at ad set level so you can make changes without resetting learning
  - Must be Conversions campaign
- **Ad set level:** Each ad set = one test
  - Test 1, Test 2, Test 3 running simultaneously
  - 3-5 ads per ad set (testing one concept)

**Testing Process (Cycle):**
1. **Run purposeful test** - Know WHY you're testing it
2. **Analysis** - Why did it fail or succeed?
3. **Reiterate** - Use results to inform next test
4. **Scale winners** - Pull successful tests into scaling campaigns (CBO, scaled ABO, or Advantage+)

**Logging requirements:**  
Track every test in a sheet. No logging = re-testing the same things over and over = no progress.

**When to use it:**  
For brands spending <$50k/month. Primary testing method for early-stage accounts.

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Test 1 (Ad Set):** Problem-solution hook + site analysis angle + 3 static variations  
**Test 2 (Ad Set):** Curiosity hook + crit feedback angle + 3 UGC videos  
**Test 3 (Ad Set):** Authority hook + student testimonials + 3 carousel ads

### 12. Hierarchy of Importance (What to Test First)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
Not all variables are equal. Test in order of impact.

**Priority order (most to least impactful):**
1. **Structure** - Account setup, campaign type, budget distribution
2. **Angle** - The pain point/benefit you're highlighting
3. **Format/Asset Type** - Video vs. static vs. carousel vs. UGC
4. **Value propositions** - Specific product benefits
5. **Targeting** - Audience selection (least important on Meta due to AI)

**Important note:** This hierarchy is account-dependent. Some accounts see biggest wins from structure changes, others from angle changes. Test to find YOUR hierarchy.

**When to use it:**  
When deciding what to test next. Don't obsess over targeting tweaks when your angle might be the real problem.

**Nathan's example:**  
"I wish I could give you a rigid answer... but it differs account to account. We've seen accounts where this has been completely flipped on its head."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Priority 1 (Structure):** Test ABO vs. Advantage+ with min spend caps  
**Priority 2 (Angle):** Test "vague crit feedback" vs. "all-nighter workflow" vs. "site analysis speed"  
**Priority 3 (Format):** Test Notes-style static vs. UGC video vs. carousel  
**Priority 4 (Value props):** Test "$29/mo" vs. "same price as lunch" vs. "cancel anytime"  
**Priority 5 (Targeting):** Let Meta optimize (don't obsess over manual targeting)

### 13. Purposeful Testing Protocol
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A checklist to ensure every test has strategic value and produces actionable data.

**Requirements for a purposeful test:**
1. **Build a hypothesis** - Know what the outcome SHOULD be before running the test
2. **Keep variables constant** - Change only ONE variable per test (otherwise data isn't statistically relevant)
3. **Log all results** - Document what was tested, outcome, and implications for next test
4. **Identify your hierarchy** - What makes the biggest impact in YOUR account? (Angle? Asset type? Structure?)

**When to use it:**  
Before launching ANY test. If you can't articulate the hypothesis and what you're controlling for, don't run the test.

**Nathan's example:**  
"There is no point in running a test if you don't know what the outcome should be from that test."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Bad test:** Launch 10 ads with different everything (can't tell what worked)  
**Good test:**  
- **Hypothesis:** Students will relate more to "vague crit feedback" angle than "all-nighter workflow" angle
- **Variable:** Angle (crit feedback vs. workflow)
- **Constants:** Same format (UGC video), same hook type (problem-solution), same CTA
- **Expected outcome:** Higher CTR and lower CPA on crit feedback angle

### 14. Strategic Creative Reiteration (The Flywheel)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A continuous loop of creating, testing, analyzing, and reiterating creative based on data.

**The flywheel process:**
1. **Create content** - Either produce volume cheaply OR predict what will work
2. **Test in account** - Run structured tests
3. **Analyze results** - What concepts/angles/formats performed?
4. **Reiterate** - Use learnings to inform next batch of creative
5. **Scale winners** - Pull winning ads into scaling campaigns
6. **Repeat**

**Two paths to success:**
- **Path A:** Create compelling content for pennies (high volume, low cost)
- **Path B:** Predict what will perform (high accuracy, lower volume)
- **Best:** Do both (high volume + high predictive accuracy)

**When to use it:**  
This is the ongoing operating system for creative, not a one-time project. Build this into your weekly/monthly cadence.

**Nathan's example:**  
"Creative equals cash. It's expensive to make new content. You need to know how to either create compelling content for pennies OR predict what will perform. Even better if you can do both."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
1. **Create:** Dan films 5 UGC videos with iPhone (low cost)
2. **Test:** Run all 5 in ABO testing campaign
3. **Analyze:** "Vague crit feedback" angle outperforms "all-nighter workflow" by 40%
4. **Reiterate:** Next batch focuses on crit-related pain points (different variations)
5. **Scale:** Move winning crit feedback ad to Advantage+ campaign
6. **Repeat:** Film next batch with new insights

### 15. Retargeting Campaign Structure (90-Day Objection Handling)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
A dedicated retargeting campaign targeting 90-day site visitors with objection-handling creative.

**Setup:**
- **Campaign:** ABO
- **Ad set:** 90-day visitors (custom audience)
  - Why 90 days? Shorter periods (30, 60 days) = smaller audience = higher CPMs
  - No stacked audiences, no exclusions (causes CPM inflation)
- **Ads:** 3-4 ads handling different objections

**Objection handling framework:**  
Analyze WHY people didn't convert (if you have 5% conversion rate, 95% didn't buy):
- 20% price-conscious → Address with discount or value anchoring
- 20% don't trust brand → Show social proof (# of customers, reviews)
- 20% don't trust product quality → UGC demonstrating product in use
- etc.

**When to use it:**  
For brands spending >$3k/month. Optional but recommended in 90% of cases.

**Nathan's example:**  
"Maybe 20% of users weren't converting because they were price conscious. Another 20% didn't trust the brand. Another 20% didn't trust product quality. Within the 3 ads we're running back to those users, we want to directly handle those objections."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Objection 1 (Price):** "Same price as two coffees at Starbucks" or "$29/mo, cancel anytime"  
**Objection 2 (Trust):** "Over 200 students, 4.8/5 rating, founded by [Dan's credentials]"  
**Objection 3 (Value):** Student testimonial showing before/after project quality or crit score improvement  
**Objection 4 (Time):** "10-minute lessons, not 2-hour lectures" (low time commitment)

### 16. Bottleneck Analysis Framework (Three Core Skills for Top 1% Marketers)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**Skill 1: Concept Building**
- Build the idea for the creative (persona + angle + offer)

### 17. Measurement Systems by Revenue Stage
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**6-figure brands:**
- **Metrics:** MER (Marketing Efficiency Ratio) and CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- **Channels:** One channel only (don't dilute focus)
- **Products:** One product (don't dilute data)
- Track daily, make decisions based on fluctuations

**7-figure brands (early):**
- **Add:** 7-day click ROAS (or 7DC + 1DV for long purchase cycles)
- **Add:** Acquisition MER (filters out returning customer revenue inflation)
- **Keep:** CAC

**7-figure brands (mid-high):**
- **Same as above** + start looking at returning customer revenue impact

**8-figure brands:**
- **Add:** Incrementality studies (multiple channels make attribution unclear)
- **Add:** LTV:CAC ratio (you have retention data now + cash reserves to invest in acquisition)
- **Keep:** 7DC ROAS and Acquisition MER

**9-figure brands:**
- **Add:** Offline conversion tracking (retail, B2B, wholesale)
- **Add:** Marketing Mix Models (MMM) or Media Mix Models
- **Add:** Brand recall studies (third-party)
- **Complexity:** CAC online vs. CAC total business; LTV online vs. LTV total

**Critical rule:** Don't apply a 9-figure measurement system to a 6-figure business (and vice versa).

**Konteks Kourse application (6-7 figure brand):**  
- **Track:** MER and CAC daily
- **One channel:** Meta only (don't test Meta + Google + TikTok simultaneously)
- **One product:** $29/mo Skool membership (don't launch multiple tiers yet)
- **Ignore:** LTV:CAC, incrementality studies, offline tracking (too early)

### 18. Why Statics > Videos for Early-Stage Brands
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
For early-stage 7-figure brands, static ads should be prioritized over videos due to production cost vs. expected value.

**The math:**
- **Static production cost:** $10-15 per ad
- **Video production cost:** $40+ per ad
- **Performance:** Similar expected value (message matters, not format)
- **Conclusion:** 4x cheaper to produce statics = 4x more testing volume = better ROI

**When to use videos:**
- When you have format diversity goals (some users only convert from videos)
- When founder is a video editor (production cost = $0)
- When product requires demonstration (complicated value prop)

**When to use statics:**
- Capital-constrained brands
- Early testing phases (maximize test volume)
- Products with clear, simple value props

**Recommendation:** 90 statics + 10 videos (not 50/50 split)

**Nathan's example:**  
"If you're constrained by capital, you need to look at the cost to make 100 videos vs. 100 statics. We'd make 90 statics and 10 videos. The videos are just to maximize reach against users who wouldn't be targeted by statics."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
- **Prioritize:** Notes-style statics (text on colored backgrounds, very cheap to produce)
- **Secondary:** Student UGC testimonials (ask students to film themselves, low cost)
- **Rare:** Professionally shot videos (only after statics prove the angle works)
- **Rationale:** Dan can design 20 statics in Canva in 2 hours ($0 cost). One professional video = $200-500.

### 19. Creative Volume & Spend Capacity Model
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
Every ad has a maximum spend threshold before it fatigues. To scale spend, you need volume + diversity.

**The model:**
- **Each ad = a unit** with a maximum spend capacity
- **Example:** Ad might max out at $1k/day spend before efficiency drops
- To spend $10k/day, you need 10+ ads performing simultaneously
- **Layering spend:** Volume + diversity allows you to layer spend while maintaining efficiency

**The danger (account leverage):**  
Brands with incredible product-market fit launch 5-10 ads/month for years. One ad hits, they stop testing (0-5 ads/month). Account becomes leveraged on 1-2 ads. When those ads fatigue, account spirals.

**Prevention:**  
Maintain consistent creative volume (even when you have winners). Never leverage the entire account on 1-2 ads.

**Nathan's example:**  
"Brands that end up in this position have incredible product-market fit. They launch 5-10 ads per month. Eventually one ad hits and scales. Testing goes down to zero because 'this is working, why test?' Then they end up with the entire account leveraged on 1-2 ads. Horrible position."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
If one ad (e.g., "Vague crit feedback decoded") hits and scales to $50/day:
- **Don't stop testing** - Keep launching 10-15 new ads/month
- **Diversify angles** - Don't only make "crit feedback" ads
- **Diversify formats** - Don't only make statics if statics are working
- **Goal:** 5-10 ads performing simultaneously at $10-20/day each = $50-200/day total

### 20. The Rich Get Richer (ML Learning Curve)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
Digital ad platforms improve efficiency as you INCREASE spend (opposite of traditional media).

**Why:**
- Every campaign has machine learning guided by conversion data
- More conversions = better data = better targeting = better efficiency
- At launch (low data), efficiency is WORST
- As spend increases (more data), efficiency IMPROVES

**The mistake:**  
Brand launches at $2k/month, gets 4 ROAS (breakeven). Waits to hit 6 ROAS before increasing spend. They never will - they need to INCREASE spend to GET to 6 ROAS.

**Traditional media (opposite):**
- Launch in perfect-fit magazine = max efficiency day 1
- Scale requires broader magazines = efficiency erodes

**Digital (opposite):**
- Launch with zero data = worst efficiency day 1
- Scale adds conversion data = efficiency improves

**When to use it:**  
When deciding whether to increase ad spend. If you're at breakeven ROAS, increasing spend (into the RIGHT campaigns) will likely improve efficiency.

**Nathan's example:**  
"You will see better efficiency if you increase spend. It seems counterintuitive, but it is how it works. Business owners don't understand this because they're comparing themselves to $10M brands that DO see erosion. You're at $30k/month - you need to spend more to get better efficiency."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
If spending $30/day at 3 ROAS (barely profitable):
- **Don't:** Wait for 5 ROAS before increasing spend
- **Do:** Increase to $50/day (more conversions = better ML = likely higher ROAS)
- **Caveat:** Only if spend is going to COLD targeting (not retargeting). More spend on retargeting = just higher % of existing visitors, not new customers.

### 21. Cold vs. Retargeting Delineation (New Customer Acquisition Truth)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
ROAS does NOT equal new customers. Agencies can easily manipulate ROAS by shifting spend to retargeting.

**The manipulation:**
- Take money out of cold, put in retargeting = ROAS doubles (looks great)
- Increase PMAG budgets, remove exclusions from cold = more retargeting (looks like cold)
- Run retargeting campaign next to Advantage+ = suppresses retargeting in A+ = A+ does more cold (forced delineation)

**The truth:**
- Running retargeting NEXT TO Advantage+ actually HELPS cold targeting
- Retargeting campaigns bid higher (on warm audiences) → take priority at placement
- This FORCES Advantage+ to target cold audiences (can't serve warm audiences because retargeting already claimed them)

**When to use it:**  
When evaluating account performance or agency performance. Don't just look at ROAS - look at new customer acquisition rate.

**Nathan's example:**

### 24. Don't Turn Ads Off in Performing Campaigns (The Breakdown Effect)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau]**

**What it is:**  
If a campaign/ad set is hitting KPI, do NOT go in and turn off "underperforming" ads within it.

**Why:**
- Optimization occurs at the CAMPAIGN level, not ad level
- You only see last-click attribution, but Meta optimizes across multi-touch attribution
- An ad spending $5k/month at 3 ROAS might be part of a sequence where it's shown first, then another ad gets the conversion
- Turning it off resets ML and likely DECREASES campaign performance

**When you CAN turn ads off:**
- Campaign is NOT hitting KPI (do whatever you want to try to fix it)
- You're testing something to see if it improves performance
- But don't expect it to be a magic fix - there are usually better levers to pull

**Nathan's example:**  
"If you have a campaign with 3 ads spending $5k each at 5, 4, and 3 ROAS, intuitively you'd turn off the 3 ROAS ad. But the campaign is optimizing for best possible CPA across all ads. If it's spending on the 3 ROAS ad, there's a REASON - either it can't spend more on the top two without efficiency dropping, OR the 3 ROAS ad is part of a multi-touch sequence."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
If Advantage+ campaign has 5 ads, and one is at 2 ROAS while others are at 4 ROAS, but CAMPAIGN is at 3.5 ROAS (above KPI):
- **Don't:** Turn off the 2 ROAS ad
- **Do:** Let Meta continue optimizing spend distribution
- **Only turn off if:** Campaign drops below KPI and you want to test if removing it helps

### 25. Reverse-Scripting Framework (Education-First Ad Structure)
**[PRIMARY - Nathan Perdriau - Instagram Reel Critique]**

**What it is:**  
Don't lead with the product value proposition. Lead with education about the problem, THEN present the solution.

**Bad structure:**  
"Our new case covers the button that can break. The button costs $750 to replace." (product-first)

**Good structure:**  
"Did you know this part of the iPhone 17 costs $750 to replace when it smashes? [PROBLEM EDUCATION] Most cases leave it exposed. [PROBLEM AGITATION] Here's the solution." (education-first, product-second)

**Why it works:**
- Feels like organic educational content, not an ad
- Builds problem awareness before offering solution
- Correct order: Problem → Agitation → Solution (not Solution → Justification)

**When to use it:**  
Video ad scripts where you want to maximize watch time and trust.

**Nathan's example (Mass Design phone case ad):**  
"Most people would open with the value prop: 'our case covers the button.' Instead, this ad educates like organic content: 'did you know the button costs $750 to replace?' THEN presents the solution. That's the correct order."

**Konteks Kourse application:**  
**Bad:** "Konteks Kourse teaches you how to decode crit feedback. Join for $29/mo."  
**Good:** "You know when your prof says 'make it more architectural' and you have NO idea what that means? [PROBLEM] Here's what they're actually asking for: [EDUCATION - teach the framework]. This is exactly what we break down in Konteks Kourse."

### Content-to-Ad Conversion System

**Step 1: Identify High-Performing Organic Content**
- Check Instagram Reels/TikTok analytics
- Look for: High watch time, high engagement, saves/shares
- Content that "teaches something" > content that "entertains"

**Step 2: Extract the Core Concept**
- What is the persona? (who engaged with it)
- What is the angle? (what pain point or benefit did it address)
- What is the format? (talking head, B-roll, text-on-screen, etc.)

**Step 3: Adapt for Paid**
- **Tighten the hook:** First 3 seconds must be more aggressive (organic can be slower)
- **Add product bridge:** Organic content doesn't need to sell - paid does. Add 10-15 second bridge to product.
- **Clarify CTA:** Organic = engagement CTA ("follow for more"). Paid = conversion CTA ("join now").

**Step 4: Create Variations**
- **Hook variations:** Same body, 5-10 different hooks (test which grabs attention best)
- **Format variations:** Turn video into static carousel, static into video, etc.
- **Angle variations:** Same format, different pain point addressed

**Step 5: Test in Structured Framework**
- Launch in ABO testing campaign (3-5 variations per ad set)
- KPI at ad set level (not individual ad level)
- Analyze: Which concept/angle performed? (not just which individual ad)

**Step 6: Scale Winners**
- Pull winning concepts into Advantage+ scaling campaign
- Continue iterating on losing concepts (change format or angle, re-test)
