### Page 2: Shortcut #1 - The Grading Rubric Hack

**What nobody tells you:** Every architecture school uses a grading rubric. Most students never see it. Those who do rarely know how to use it strategically.

**The shortcut:**
1. Ask your tutor for the grading rubric on Day 1 of every project. If they don't have one, ask what criteria they'll assess on. (They always have criteria - they just don't volunteer it.)
2. Print it out. Highlight the top-weighted criteria.
3. For each criterion, write ONE sentence about how your project addresses it.
4. Before your crit, check each criterion has a visible answer in your presentation.

**Why this works:** Most students design based on what THEY think matters. Top students design based on what the ASSESSORS are looking for. This isn't about gaming the system - it's about not losing marks on things you could easily address.

**Time to implement:** 15 minutes per project.

### Page 3: Shortcut #2 - The 3-Concept Rule

**What nobody tells you:** Your first concept idea is almost never your best one. But most students commit to it because starting over feels like wasted time.

**The shortcut:**
1. After reading the brief, develop THREE completely different concept directions. Not variations of one idea - three genuinely different approaches.
2. For each, write: (a) one sentence describing the concept, (b) one precedent project that did something similar, (c) one reason this concept is specifically relevant to THIS site/brief.
3. Present all three to your tutor in the next desk crit. Ask which has the most potential.
4. Develop the strongest one. Keep the other two as backup if your tutor redirects you later.

**Why this works:** Having three options removes the emotional attachment to one idea. It lets your tutor guide you toward the strongest direction instead of trying to fix a weak one. It also shows design thinking - which is what crits are actually assessing.

**Time to implement:** 2 hours at the start of each project (saves 20+ hours of rework later).

### Page 4: Shortcut #3 - The Crit Presentation Formula

**What nobody tells you:** Most students present their projects chronologically (I started here, then I did this, then I changed this). This is the worst possible format for a crit.

**The shortcut - Present in this order:**
1. **The problem** (30 seconds): What challenge does the brief/site present? Why is this site/context interesting?
2. **Your position** (30 seconds): What is your concept, in one sentence? What's the ONE big idea?
3. **The evidence** (2 minutes): Show how the concept manifests in your design decisions. Site plan, floor plans, sections, diagrams - each one supporting the concept.
4. **The question** (15 seconds): End with a specific question for the panel. "I'm trying to resolve the transition between public and private space on the ground floor - I'd appreciate your input on that."

**Why this works:** The chronological approach forces critics to wait for the point. They get bored or confused and fill the silence with vague feedback ("push it further"). The formula gives them your concept upfront so they can evaluate the work against YOUR stated intention. The ending question directs their feedback to something useful instead of leaving it open for random criticism.

**Time to implement:** 30 minutes of re-ordering your presentation.

### Page 5: Shortcut #4 - Site Analysis That Actually Means Something

**What nobody tells you:** A sun path diagram, wind rose, and site photos is NOT a site analysis. It's data collection. The analysis happens when you say what the data MEANS for your design.

**The shortcut:**
1. For every piece of site data you collect, write ONE design implication.
   - "Prevailing winds from the southwest" becomes "Living spaces should be oriented northeast to avoid wind exposure, OR deliberately use wind for natural ventilation in summer."
   - "Adjacent building is 4 storeys" becomes "Ground floor will be in shadow from 2pm onwards - use upper floors for daylight-dependent spaces."
2. Format your site analysis page as: LEFT = data/diagram, RIGHT = design implication.
3. At the end, write 3 site-driven design principles that will guide your project.

**Why this works:** Lecturers have seen 10,000 sun path diagrams. They don't care about the diagram - they care about whether you can turn data into decisions. This format proves you can think, not just collect.

**Time to implement:** Add 1 hour to your existing site analysis.

### Page 6: Shortcut #5 - The Weekly Planning System

**What nobody tells you:** All-nighters are a symptom of bad planning, not hard work. The students pulling all-nighters aren't working harder than you - they started their final drawings too late.

**The shortcut:**
1. When you get a project brief, immediately count the weeks to submission.
2. Block the LAST week for final production only (printing, model photos, layout). No design changes.
3. Block the SECOND-TO-LAST week for final drawing production. All design decisions locked.
4. Everything else is design development. Work backwards from the production weeks.
5. Every Sunday night, write 3 things you need to finish by Friday. Just 3. If you finish them, add more. If you don't finish them, they carry over.

**Why this works:** Architecture students don't run out of time. They run out of production time. The design phase expands to fill all available space, leaving no time for quality output. Blocking production time first means you always submit finished work, even if the design isn't perfect. Finished-and-decent beats ambitious-and-unfinished every single time.

**Time to implement:** 20 minutes on the first Sunday of every project.
