### Page 1: Introduction

You know that person in studio who just... gets it?

### Page 2: Shortcut #1 - The Grading Rubric Hack

**What nobody tells you:** Every architecture school uses a grading rubric. Most students never see it. The ones who do rarely use it properly.

**The shortcut:**
1. Ask your tutor for the grading rubric on Day 1 of every project. If they don't have one, ask what criteria they'll use to assess. (They always have criteria. They just don't hand them out.)
2. Print it. Highlight the top-weighted criteria.
3. For each criterion, write ONE sentence about how your project addresses it.
4. Before your crit, check each criterion has a visible answer in your presentation.

**Why this works:** Most students design based on what they think matters. The students getting top marks design based on what the assessors are looking for. You're not gaming anything. You're just not losing marks on stuff you could easily fix.

**Time to implement:** 15 minutes per project.

### Page 3: Shortcut #2 - The 3-Concept Rule

**What nobody tells you:** Your first concept idea is almost never your best. But most students commit to it because starting over feels like wasted time.

**The shortcut:**
1. After reading the brief, come up with THREE different concept directions. Not variations of one idea. Three genuinely different approaches.
2. For each, write: (a) one sentence describing the concept, (b) one precedent project that did something similar, (c) one reason this concept fits THIS site or brief specifically.
3. Present all three to your tutor at the next desk crit. Ask which has legs.
4. Develop the strongest one. Keep the other two as backup if you get redirected later.

**Why this works:** Three options kills the emotional attachment to one idea. Your tutor can guide you toward the strongest direction instead of trying to fix a weak one. And it shows design thinking, which is what crits are actually assessing.

**Time to implement:** 2 hours at the start of each project (saves 20+ hours of rework later).

### Page 4: Shortcut #3 - The Crit Presentation Formula

**What nobody tells you:** Most students present chronologically (I started here, then I did this, then I changed this). Worst possible format.

**The shortcut - present in this order:**
1. **The problem** (30 seconds): What challenge does the brief or site present? Why is this interesting?
2. **Your position** (30 seconds): What is your concept, in one sentence? The ONE big idea.
3. **The evidence** (2 minutes): Show how the concept shows up in your design. Site plan, sections, diagrams - each one supporting the concept.
4. **The question** (15 seconds): End with something specific for the panel. "I'm trying to resolve the transition between public and private on the ground floor. I'd appreciate your input."

**Why this works:** The chronological approach forces your critics to sit there waiting for the point. They get bored and fill the silence with vague stuff like "push it further." This formula gives them the concept upfront so they evaluate the work against what you're actually trying to do. The question at the end directs their feedback somewhere useful.

**Time to implement:** 30 minutes of re-ordering your presentation.

### Page 5: Shortcut #4 - Site Analysis That Means Something

**What nobody tells you:** A sun path diagram, wind rose and some site photos is data collection. The analysis is when you say what the data means for your design.

**The shortcut:**
1. For every piece of site data, write ONE design implication.
   - "Prevailing winds from the southwest" becomes "Orient living spaces northeast, or deliberately use the wind for cross-ventilation in summer."
   - "Adjacent building is 4 storeys" becomes "Ground floor in shadow from 2pm. Put daylight-dependent spaces upstairs."
2. Format your page as: LEFT = data/diagram, RIGHT = what it means for the design.
3. At the end, write 3 site-driven design principles that will guide your project.

**Why this works:** Your lecturer has seen 10,000 sun path diagrams. The diagram isn't the point. The point is whether you can turn data into design decisions. This layout proves you can.

**Time to implement:** Add 1 hour to your existing site analysis.

### Page 6: Shortcut #5 - The Weekly Planning System

**What nobody tells you:** All-nighters happen because of bad planning, not because the work is too hard. The students pulling all-nighters started their final drawings too late.

**The shortcut:**
1. When you get a project brief, count the weeks to submission.
2. Block the LAST week for production only. Printing, model photos, layout. No design changes.
3. Block the SECOND-TO-LAST week for final drawings. All design decisions locked by then.
4. Everything before that is design development. Work backwards from the production weeks.
5. Every Sunday night, write down 3 things to finish by Friday. Just 3. Finish those, then add more.

**Why this works:** You never actually run out of time. You run out of production time. Design expands to fill whatever space you give it, then there's nothing left for output. Blocking production time first means you always hand in finished work. Finished-and-decent beats ambitious-and-unfinished every time in architecture school.

**Time to implement:** 20 minutes on the first Sunday of every project.
